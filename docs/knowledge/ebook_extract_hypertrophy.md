## 1. Volume Landmarks Framework (Core Model)

### MEV, MAV, MRV System
- **Framework:** Volume Landmarks — defines the three critical thresholds for hypertrophy training
- **Key Data:**
  - **MEV (Minimum Effective Volume):** The lowest volume at which you can still produce hypertrophy stimulus
  - **MAV (Maximally Adaptive Volume):** The sweet spot where gains are optimized — training at this level produces best results
  - **MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume):** The upper limit of volume your recovery capacity can handle — beyond this causes excessive fatigue and decreases gains
- **Protocol:** Train between MEV and MRV, ideally targeting MAV for each muscle group
- **Key Nuance:** Different muscle groups have different MEV/MAV/MRV ranges. The "sweet spot" is NOT at the edges — "flirting on the edges in either particular direction" is suboptimal. Train in the middle zone.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Volume Landmarks (foundational concept throughout entire book)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `periodization_progression.md` — volume planning; `training_splits.md` — per-muscle-group volume allocation

## 2. Sets Per Muscle Group Per Week

- **Framework:** Weekly volume prescription based on muscle group and training experience
- **Key Data:**
  - **Beginner:** 10-15 sets per muscle group per week (total volume across all exercises for that muscle)
  - **Intermediate:** 15-20 sets per muscle group per week
  - **Advanced:** 20+ sets per muscle group per week (may require 25-30+ for lagging muscles)
  - **Deload/Recovery weeks:** Reduce volume by 40-50% of normal weekly load
- **Protocol:**
  - Count all sets for a target muscle group (e.g., all chest exercises = total chest sets for week)
  - Adjust based on individual response — if lagging, slowly raise set count; if overreached, slowly lower
  - Test by slowly raising volume and observing growth response
- **Key Nuance:** "The sweet spot is to be between your MEV and MRV, not flirting on the edges in either particular direction." More volume is NOT always better. Some muscles respond better to lower volume than expected.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Training Volume (pp. 10-15)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `periodization_progression.md` — weekly macrocycle planning; `training_splits.md` — split design with volume allocation

## 3. SFR (Stimulus-to-Fatigue Ratio) & RSM (Relative Stimulus Magnitude)

### SFR Framework
- **Framework:** SFR = the ratio of hypertrophic stimulus to cumulative fatigue generated by an exercise
- **Key Data:**
  - **High SFR exercise:** Produces lots of stimulus with minimal fatigue (e.g., leg press for quads at high reps)
  - **Low SFR exercise:** Produces stimulus but with high fatigue cost (e.g., barbell back squats for some individuals)
  - **SFR varies by individual:** Same exercise can be high SFR for one person, low for another
- **Protocol:**
  - Prioritize high-SFR exercises — they allow more volume with better recovery
  - Use low-SFR exercises sparingly or in lower volumes
  - Select movements that give you high SFRs AND highest RSMs (receptor stimulus magnitudes) for lagging muscles
  - Rotate exercises if SFR drops — don't just "go through the motions"
- **Key Nuance:** "Don't just go through the motions! If an exercise has a low SFR for you, try another one." One exercise is NOT equally effective for all people. Be willing to experiment.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Exercise Selection (pp. 18-22)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `exercise_database.md` — per-exercise SFR ratings and muscle-group mapping; `training_splits.md` — exercise selection logic

## 4. RSM (Relative Stimulus Magnitude) Concept

- **Framework:** RSM = how effectively an exercise stimulates a target muscle relative to other exercises
- **Key Data:**
  - Highest RSM exercises are "best weapons for the battle" — prioritize these
  - RSM can vary by individual leverage, ROM (range of motion), and technique
  - Example contrast: Leg press (high RSM for quads) vs. leg press sets of 5-10 reps (poor quad stimulus, may only hurt knees)
- **Protocol:**
  - For lagging muscle groups, use exercises with the highest RSMs
  - Pair high SFR + high RSM exercises as primary work
  - Test different rep ranges to find the RSM sweet spot for each muscle
- **Key Nuance:** "Be logical in your exercise selection, and always be willing to make changes to your plan as certain exercises ebb and flow between varied levels of perceived effectiveness."
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Exercise Selection (pp. 18-22)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `exercise_database.md` — RSM rankings by muscle group; `training_splits.md` — primary vs. accessory exercise selection

## 5. Rep Range Specificity for Muscle Groups

- **Framework:** Different muscles respond optimally to different rep ranges due to fiber type distribution and biomechanics
- **Key Data:**
  - **Some muscles (e.g., hamstrings, glutes):** Achieve very high SFRs with heavy weight (low reps)
  - **Other muscles (e.g., quads, chest):** Achieve high SFRs with lighter weight and higher reps
  - **Example:** Leg presses for sets of 5-10 reps may only hurt knees; sets of 20 reps may absolutely destroy quads in the best way possible
- **Protocol:**
  - Test multiple rep ranges for the same exercise
  - Identify which rep range produces the most pumps, burns, fatigue, and soreness for a given muscle
  - Bias future training toward the rep ranges that seem most effective
  - Be willing to explore — you might get better growth at much smaller or much larger volumes than expected
- **Key Nuance:** Erroneous exercise dismissal: "If you've never tried the higher rep range in this case, you might have erroneously written off the leg press as a low SFR exercise." Pre-judgment based on limited testing is a common mistake.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Rep Range Selection (pp. 22-26)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `training_splits.md` — rep range prescription per exercise; `exercise_database.md` — optimal rep ranges by muscle group

## 6. RIR (Reps in Reserve) for Hypertrophy

- **Framework:** RIR = estimated reps you could perform before reaching concentric failure
- **Key Data:**
  - **Optimal for hypertrophy:** RIR 0-3 (stopping 0-3 reps short of failure)
  - **Too close to failure (RIR 0):** Maximizes stimulus but increases fatigue and joint stress
  - **Too far from failure (RIR 4+):** Reduces stimulus, requires more sets to reach volume targets
- **Protocol:**
  - Most hypertrophy work should be RIR 0-3
  - Beginners can stay slightly further from failure (RIR 2-3) and still make gains
  - Advanced trainees may need to go closer to failure (RIR 0-1) to generate sufficient stimulus
- **Key Nuance:** Moving closer to failure is a valid progression strategy, but it increases fatigue cost. Monitor recovery.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Intensity & RIR (pp. 26-30)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `periodization_progression.md` — intensity periodization; `training_splits.md` — RIR prescription by phase

## 7. Frequency (Sessions Per Muscle Group Per Week)

- **Framework:** How often you train each muscle group per week
- **Key Data:**
  - **Beginner:** 2-3x per muscle group per week
  - **Intermediate:** 2-4x per muscle group per week
  - **Advanced:** 3-5x per muscle group per week
  - **Optimal for most:** 2-3x per week balances stimulus and recovery
- **Protocol:**
  - Higher frequency allows you to spread volume across more sessions (lower fatigue per session, better recovery)
  - Lower frequency concentrates volume into fewer sessions (may accumulate too much fatigue)
  - Test: if you're underreaching, increase frequency; if overreached, decrease
- **Key Nuance:** Frequency is a tool to manage both stimulus AND fatigue. Not just about hitting muscle groups more often.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Training Frequency (pp. 30-34)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `training_splits.md` — frequency design; `periodization_progression.md` — frequency periodization

## 8. Exercise Selection Criteria (Detailed Hierarchy)

### Tier 1 — Primary/Compound Lifts (High SFR + High RSM)
- **Examples:** Barbell squat, barbell bench press, barbell deadlift (for posterior chain), leg press (for quads)
- **Protocol:** 3-5 sets per exercise, RIR 0-3, primary position in session
- **Data:** These generate the most stimulus per unit of time and allow heaviest loads

### Tier 2 — Secondary Compounds (Moderate SFR + High RSM)
- **Examples:** Incline press, hack squat, leg press (as secondary), leg curl machines
- **Protocol:** 3-4 sets per exercise, RIR 0-3, after primary work
- **Data:** Still mechanically demanding but slightly lower fatigue cost

### Tier 3 — Isolation/Accessories (High SFR + Moderate RSM)
- **Examples:** Machine flyes, cable movements, dumbbell isolation, plate-loaded machines
- **Protocol:** 2-4 sets per exercise, RIR 1-3, flexibility in positioning
- **Data:** Lower systemic fatigue, can accumulate higher total volume

### Selection Logic:
- **For strong muscle:** Use whatever maintains volume baseline (could be low-volume compounds)
- **For lagging muscle:** Use high-SFR + high-RSM combination; prioritize exercises that produce best pumps/soreness
- **Variation:** Change exercises every 4-12 weeks to prevent accommodation and find new SFR/RSM relationships
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Exercise Selection (pp. 18-22)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `exercise_database.md` — exercise tier classification; `training_splits.md` — exercise ordering and pairing

## 9. Training Split Types & Recommendations

- **Framework:** How to distribute volume and exercises across training days
- **Key Data:**
  - **Upper/Lower (2x per week frequency):** 2 upper, 2 lower sessions; moderate frequency, allows high volume per session
  - **Push/Pull/Legs (2x per week frequency):** 6 days total; good for intermediate+; spreads volume
  - **Full Body (3x per week frequency):** All muscles 3x/week; excellent recovery management, great for beginners
  - **Body Part Splits (1-2x per week frequency):** Traditional bodybuilding; higher volume per session, lower frequency (recovery intensive)
- **Protocol:**
  - Match split to experience level and recovery capacity
  - Lower frequency splits require better fatigue management
  - Higher frequency splits allow better recovery but require shorter sessions
- **Key Nuance:** No split is inherently superior — fit the split to the individual's recovery, schedule, and response
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Training Splits (pp. 34-40)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `training_splits.md` — split templates and periodization; `periodization_progression.md` — split progression by phase

## 10. Progressive Overload Strategies (Beyond Just Adding Weight)

- **Framework:** Multiple variables can create progression; weight is only one
- **Key Data:**
  - **Volume progression:** Add sets, increase reps at same weight, increase frequency
  - **Intensity progression:** Reduce RIR, add weight, increase density (same volume in less time)
  - **Exercise progression:** Improve SFR or RSM by changing exercise, ROM, or leverage
  - **Fatigue management progression:** Reduce deload length, increase inter-session recovery
- **Protocol:**
  - Track multiple metrics: weight, reps, sets, RPE, soreness
  - Aim to progress ONE variable per microcycle (week)
  - Don't chase weight gains if volume is constant (may indicate minimal stimulus)
  - Rotate progression focus (volume blocks → intensity blocks → technique refinement)
- **Key Nuance:** "Getting stronger doesn't guarantee you're getting bigger." Strength and size are correlated but not identical. Prioritize volume and stimulus over strength gains in hypertrophy phases.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Progressive Overload (pp. 40-44)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `periodization_progression.md` — progression schemes; `training_splits.md` — overload strategy by phase

## 11. Fatigue Management & Recovery

### Sleep & Stress
- **Framework:** Fatigue accumulation depends on total recovery, not just training
- **Key Data:**
  - **Required sleep:** 7-9 hours per night for optimal recovery
  - **Impact of poor sleep:** Reduces anabolic hormones, increases cortisol, impairs hypertrophy signaling
  - **Stress management:** High chronic stress (non-training) increases cumulative fatigue and decreases gains
  - **Best practice:** Regular unwinding time most days of the week
- **Protocol:**
  - Prioritize 7-9 hours sleep before prioritizing volume increases
  - Manage life stress (work, relationships, finances) — it directly impacts training results
  - Use active recovery and meditation if stress is high
- **Key Nuance:** "If you're not sleeping enough to be well-rested (usually 7-9 hours per night), if intense levels of stress are killing your energy levels, and if you don't have regular times during most days to unwind, you may not be gaining at your best rates."
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Fatigue Management (pp. 44-48)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `ngx_philosophy_expanded.md` — holistic wellness integration; wellness tracking in app

### Deloads
- **Framework:** Strategic reduction in training volume and intensity to manage cumulative fatigue
- **Key Data:**
  - **Deload frequency:** Every 4-8 weeks (varies by volume and intensity)
  - **Deload protocol:** Reduce volume 40-50%, reduce intensity (increase RIR), reduce frequency if possible
  - **Deload duration:** 3-7 days typically
  - **Effect:** Allows CNS and connective tissue recovery; often produces rapid strength/size bounce-back post-deload
  - **"Instant PR formula":** Late beginner/early intermediate can deload for first time and see astonishing strength/size gains within weeks
- **Protocol:**
  - Track fatigue levels (soreness, motivation, strength plateau)
  - Implement deload when: plateau in strength, persistent soreness, lethargy, motivation loss
  - Post-deload, resume with slightly lower volume than pre-deload, then progressively add
- **Key Nuance:** Deloads are NOT lazy weeks — they're strategic recovery. Very common tool in advanced programming.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Deload Protocols (pp. 48-52)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `periodization_progression.md` — deload cycles within mesocycles; `training_splits.md` — deload week templates

## 12. Body Composition & Caloric Status

### Hypercaloric (Surplus) Training
- **Framework:** Training during a caloric surplus (gaining weight slowly and steadily)
- **Key Data:**
  - **Requirement for max hypertrophy:** Must be in caloric surplus to maximize muscle gain (cannot gain muscle efficiently in deficit)
  - **Target rate:** Gain weight slowly — 0.5-1 lb per week for natural trainees
  - **Mindset:** "Don't fall for the 'painting' folly. Instead, gain weight slowly and steadily if you want to be bigger."
  - **Body weight reference:** "If you want to be jacked, and all of the lifters you look up to that are jacked weigh over 180 pounds, there's really no way you're going to wind up looking jacked if you only weigh 160 pounds."
  - **Minimum for visible muscularity:** ~180 lbs (varies by height and genetics)
- **Protocol:**
  - Consume 300-500 kcal above maintenance
  - Gain 0.5-1 lb per week
  - Track body weight weekly; if gain stalls, add 100-200 kcal
  - Prioritize protein intake (0.7-1 g per lb bodyweight)
- **Key Nuance:** Physique transformation requires accepting weight gain. There is no "getting jacked at low bodyweight" without drugs. Surplus is non-negotiable for hypertrophy.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Nutrition & Body Composition (pp. 52-58)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `ngx_philosophy_expanded.md` — nutrition science integration; Fuel agent knowledge base

### Hypocaloric (Deficit) Training
- **Framework:** Training during caloric deficit (losing weight)
- **Key Data:**
  - **Rate:** Lose weight at 0.5-1 lb per week
  - **Minimum protein:** 1 g per lb bodyweight (critical to preserve muscle in deficit)
  - **Hypertrophy goal:** Very limited in deficit; goal is preservation + maybe minimal growth in new lifters
  - **Volume consideration:** May need to reduce training volume slightly in deficit to manage recovery
- **Protocol:**
  - Maintain high protein intake
  - Reduce sets gradually if fatigue accumulates
  - Focus on strength maintenance (RIR 0-3 still applies)
  - Use shorter rest periods or higher frequency to maintain stimulus with lower fatigue
- **Key Nuance:** You cannot build significant muscle in a deficit. Accept muscle loss as natural and minimize it via protein and intelligently reduced volume.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Nutrition & Body Composition (pp. 52-58)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `ngx_philosophy_expanded.md` — seasonal periodization (bulk/cut phases); Fuel agent knowledge base

## 13. Troubleshooting Lack of Progress (8-Point System)

The book presents an 8-point diagnostic hierarchy for "not making gains":

### 1. Not in Appropriate Volume Range
- **Diagnosis:** Check if training volume (sets/week per muscle) is between MEV and MRV
- **Fix:** Adjust volume upward if below MEV; adjust downward if above MRV
- **Data:** Use the "slowly raise the number of sets and see where growth is best" approach

### 2. Not Using High-SFR Exercises
- **Diagnosis:** Exercises producing high fatigue but low stimulus
- **Fix:** Replace low-SFR exercises with high-SFR alternatives; test different rep ranges
- **Example:** Swap barbell back squat (low SFR individual) for leg press (high SFR individual)

### 3. Poor Fatigue Management (Sleep, Stress)
- **Diagnosis:** Insufficient sleep (< 7-9 hrs), high life stress, no unwinding time
- **Fix:** Increase sleep, reduce life stressors, schedule recovery time
- **Data:** This is often the hidden culprit in plateaus

### 4. Low-SFR Exercises with High Muscle Stimulus (RSM)
- **Diagnosis:** Using suboptimal exercises for lagging muscles
- **Fix:** Identify high-SFR + high-RSM combination for lagging muscle
- **Example:** If hamstring growth is stuck, use hamstring curls (very high SFR + RSM) instead of low-SFR exercises

### 5. Missing Rep Range Optimization
- **Diagnosis:** Using only one rep range, haven't tested alternatives
- **Fix:** Test multiple rep ranges; identify which produces best pumps/stimulus for each muscle
- **Example:** Leg presses at 5-10 reps might not work; 20 reps might be better
- **Protocol:** "Be willing to explore multiple rep ranges across your movement patterns in order to identify which ones will be the most conducive for hypertrophy."

### 6. Not Hypercaloric (Caloric Surplus)
- **Diagnosis:** Trying to build muscle in maintenance or deficit
- **Fix:** Enter consistent caloric surplus; gain 0.5-1 lb per week
- **Data:** "Unless, of course, you're in a hypercaloric condition and are actually gaining net tissue, you are slowly to 180 pounds!"

### 7. Poor Fatigue Management (Deloads)
- **Diagnosis:** Accumulated training fatigue from no strategic deloads
- **Fix:** Implement 3-7 day deload every 4-8 weeks (reduce volume 40-50%)
- **Data:** "A very common 'instant PR' formula is to take a late beginner or early intermediate and have them do a deload or active rest for the first time. A few weeks later, they're almost always making astonishing strength and size gains."

### 8. Insufficient Duration of Changes
- **Diagnosis:** Tried changes for only days/weeks; need longer testing period
- **Fix:** Implement changes for months — give adaptations time to manifest
- **Protocol:** "If you really want the best results, implement some of these troubleshooting tips for months and it's very likely your gains will return."
- **Key Nuance:** "It's easy to say you tried the changes but they 'didn't work' if you only tried to implement them for a few days or weeks."

- **Source Context:** Chapter on Troubleshooting (pp. 56-60)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `ngx_philosophy_expanded.md` — diagnostic framework; Fuel + Track agent logic

## 14. Protein & Macro Fundamentals

- **Framework:** Basic nutritional requirements for hypertrophy
- **Key Data:**
  - **Protein requirement:** 0.7-1.0 g per lb bodyweight
  - **Carbs:** 2-5 g per lb bodyweight (varies by activity, metabolism)
  - **Fats:** 0.3-0.5 g per lb bodyweight
  - **Caloric surplus:** 300-500 kcal above maintenance for 0.5-1 lb/week gain
  - **Hydration:** Drink to thirst; adequate hydration improves performance and recovery
- **Protocol:**
  - Track macros for 2-3 weeks to establish baseline
  - Adjust calories based on weekly bodyweight trend
  - Prioritize protein consistency (hit daily target)
  - Don't obsess over macro ratio — surplus + protein are the critical variables
- **Key Nuance:** Nutrition doesn't need to be perfect, but it needs to be adequate and consistent
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Nutrition (pp. 52-58)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `ngx_philosophy_expanded.md` — macro science; Fuel agent meal planning

## 15. Individual Variation & Personalization

- **Framework:** The concept that all recommendations are population averages; individual response varies significantly
- **Key Data:**
  - **MEV/MAV/MRV ranges vary widely:** One person's sweet spot is another's overreach
  - **SFR/RSM are individual:** Same exercise different for different people
  - **Rep range response:** Some muscles thrive on heavy/low reps, others on light/high reps — individual-specific
  - **Recovery capacity:** Sleep, stress management, genetics all create individual variation
  - **Example:** "For all you know, they might benefit from much more volume than you're currently training them with, and no amount of extra intensity will make up for that."
- **Protocol:**
  - Treat all recommendations as starting points
  - Test and adjust based on personal response
  - Track metrics: strength, aesthetics, soreness, energy
  - Be willing to deviate from general guidelines if your data shows better results
  - Document what works for YOU (not what worked for the influencer)
- **Key Nuance:** "If you notice you're experiencing crazy pumps, burns and fatigue from a given rep range and not as much from other rep ranges for a certain muscle, do the logical thing and bias a bit more of your training toward the rep range that seems most effective."
- **Source Context:** Woven throughout entire book, especially Exercise Selection and Troubleshooting chapters

## 16. Intensity Techniques (Advanced)

- **Framework:** Methods to increase training stimulus without increasing volume
- **Key Data:**
  - **Drop sets:** Reduce weight by 10-20% after reaching RIR 0, continue for more reps
  - **Rest-pause sets:** Reach RIR 0, rest 15-30 sec, continue for more reps at same weight
  - **Supersets:** Back-to-back exercises for same or synergistic muscles
  - **Cluster sets:** Multiple mini-sets with short rest (e.g., 3x3 with 20 sec rest between)
- **Protocol:**
  - Use sparingly (1-2 exercises per session max)
  - Best for secondary/tertiary movements
  - Increases fatigue substantially — reduce adjacent volume
  - Test for 3-4 weeks before evaluating
- **Key Nuance:** Higher intensity techniques increase fatigue cost. Only useful if volume is already adequate.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Intensity Techniques (pp. 40-44)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `training_splits.md` — finisher techniques; `periodization_progression.md` — intensity block protocols

## 17. Periodization Phases (Conceptual)

- **Framework:** Rotating emphasis on volume, intensity, and recovery throughout training cycle
- **Key Data:**
  - **Hypertrophy Phase (8-12 weeks):** High volume, RIR 0-3, moderate intensity, 2-3x frequency per muscle
  - **Strength Phase (4-6 weeks):** Moderate volume, heavy loads (RIR 0-1), lower reps, compound focus
  - **Deload Phase (3-7 days):** 40-50% volume reduction, higher RIR, recovery focus
  - **Transition Phase (1-2 weeks):** Exercise variation, technique refinement, active recovery
- **Protocol:**
  - Typical periodized year: Hypertrophy (8-12 wks) → Strength (6 wks) → Deload (7 days) → repeat
  - Or: Hypertrophy (12 wks) → Deload (7 days) → Hypertrophy (12 wks) → repeat
  - Adjust based on response
- **Key Nuance:** Periodization is NOT mandatory for beginners; linear progression can work. Becomes important at intermediate+.
- **Source Context:** Chapter on Training Variables (pp. 44-52)
- **Maps to Knowledge Store:** `periodization_progression.md` — phase templates; `training_splits.md` — phase-specific prescription

## 18. Key Contradictions & Nuances from Common Gym Wisdom

### "More Volume is Always Better"
- **Contradiction:** False — volume above MRV decreases gains and increases injury risk
- **Nuance:** There is an optimal volume range (MEV-MAV-MRV); above MRV you're accumulating pure fatigue with no stimulus benefit
- **Source:** Volume Landmarks chapter (pp. 10-15)

### "You Must Go Heavy to Build Muscle"
- **Contradiction:** False — rep range is flexible; many muscles respond better to higher reps
- **Nuance:** "Doing leg presses for sets of 5-10 might just hurt your knees and never really seem to stimulate your quads much. On the other hand, doing leg presses for sets of 20 might absolutely destroy your quads in the best way possible."
- **Source:** Rep Range Specificity section (pp. 22-26)

### "All Exercises Are Equally Effective"
- **Contradiction:** False — SFR and RSM vary significantly by exercise and individual
- **Nuance:** Same exercise can be high-SFR for one person and low-SFR for another; individual testing required
- **Source:** Exercise Selection chapter (pp. 18-22)

### "You Can't Build Muscle Without a Surplus"
- **Contradiction:** Partially true — very minimal hypertrophy possible in deficit, but new lifters can build some muscle
- **Nuance:** Maximized hypertrophy requires surplus; in deficit, focus on preservation + some minimal growth
- **Source:** Nutrition chapter (pp. 52-58)

### "Deloads Are for Weak/Lazy People"
- **Contradiction:** False — deloads are strategic recovery tools used by advanced trainees
- **Nuance:** "A very common 'instant PR' formula is to take a late beginner or early intermediate and have them do a deload or active rest for the first time. A few weeks later, they're almost always making astonishing strength and size gains."
- **Source:** Fatigue Management chapter (pp. 48-52)

### "Training Frequency Doesn't Matter"
- **Contradiction:** False — frequency affects volume distribution and recovery management
- **Nuance:** Higher frequency allows better fatigue management; lower frequency concentrates stimulus/fatigue. Individual capacity varies.
- **Source:** Training Frequency section (pp. 30-34)

## 19. Quick Reference: Beginner Training Prescription

- **Volume:** 10-15 sets per muscle group per week
- **Frequency:** 2-3x per muscle group per week
- **Intensity:** RIR 1-3 (can be slightly further from failure than intermediate)
- **Exercises:** Mix of compound + isolation; prioritize high-SFR + high-RSM
- **Progression:** Add weight weekly if possible; increase reps; increase sets every 2-4 weeks
- **Rep Ranges:** Test 6-15 reps; identify best pump/stimulus for each muscle
- **Frequency:** Full-body 3x/week OR Upper/Lower 2x each
- **Sleep:** 7-9 hours
- **Nutrition:** Caloric surplus (300-500 kcal above maintenance); 0.7-1g protein per lb
- **Deload:** Not yet necessary; use when needed (every 8-12 weeks once advanced)

---

## 20. Quick Reference: Intermediate Training Prescription

- **Volume:** 15-20 sets per muscle group per week
- **Frequency:** 2-4x per muscle group per week (adjust based on response)
- **Intensity:** RIR 0-3 (closer to failure than beginners)
- **Exercises:** Prioritize high-SFR + high-RSM; rotate every 6-8 weeks
- **Progression:** Multiple variables (weight, reps, sets, density); progress one per week
- **Rep Ranges:** Use rep range testing; bias training toward optimal range per muscle
- **Split Options:** Upper/Lower, Push/Pull/Legs, or hypertrophy-focused body part split
- **Sleep:** 7-9 hours (non-negotiable)
- **Nutrition:** Caloric surplus (300-500 kcal); 0.8-1g protein per lb
- **Deload:** Every 6-8 weeks (3-7 days, 40-50% volume reduction)
- **Fatigue Management:** Monitor sleep, stress, and motivation; implement deload when plateau hits

---

## 21. Quick Reference: Advanced Training Prescription

- **Volume:** 20-30+ sets per muscle group per week (varies widely by individual)
- **Frequency:** 3-5x per muscle group per week (spreads volume, manages fatigue)
- **Intensity:** Varies by phase (8-12 wks hypertrophy at RIR 0-2, then strength phase at RIR 0-1)
- **Exercises:** High individual variation; use SFR/RSM testing to customize
- **Progression:** Periodized (hypertrophy → strength → deload → repeat); multiple progression variables
- **Rep Ranges:** Highly customized per muscle; test extensively
- **Split:** Customized based on weak points; may use upper/lower, PPL, or upper/lower/legs/arms/back
- **Sleep:** 8-9 hours (critical at this level)
- **Nutrition:** Surplus or maintenance depending on goals; 1g+ protein per lb
- **Deload:** Every 4-6 weeks (more frequent due to higher volumes)
- **Fatigue Management:** Active monitoring of CNS fatigue, connective tissue health, motivation; may use daily assessments
