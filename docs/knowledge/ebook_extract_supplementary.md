## Section 1: Injury Prevention & Management Frameworks

### 1.1 Injury Prevention Foundations

**Key Data:**
- Injury prevention requires integration of training design, load management, and individual risk assessment
- Training modifications should be individualized based on mechanism of injury and athlete capacity
- Decision criteria exist for determining when to continue training vs when to modify or pause

**Protocol:**
- Assess injury mechanism: distinguish between acute trauma and chronic overuse patterns
- Implement load management strategies during training phases
- Monitor pain responses and movement quality during recovery
- Apply progressive return-to-activity protocols based on tissue healing timelines
- Use exercise selection modifications rather than complete cessation when possible

**Risk Factors for Injury Include:**
- Rapid increases in training volume or intensity (exceeding 10% weekly increases)
- Inadequate recovery periods between high-intensity sessions
- Poor movement quality under fatigue
- Insufficient warm-up or mobility work
- History of previous injury in same area
- Muscle imbalances or mobility restrictions

**When to Continue Training vs Modify:**
- **Continue with modifications:** Pain that is mild, localized, and doesn't worsen with activity; movements where pain-free ranges exist
- **Modify significantly or pause:** Sharp, acute pain; pain that worsens progressively; loss of movement quality; neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling); swelling or instability
- **Red flags requiring medical evaluation:** Severe pain, inability to bear weight, visible deformity, persistent symptoms beyond 2 weeks without improvement

### 1.2 Rehabilitation Protocols for Common Training Injuries

**Key Data:**
- Rehabilitation follows tissue-specific healing timelines
- Early intervention prevents secondary compensatory patterns
- Progressive loading accelerates functional recovery
- Return-to-sport protocols require both strength and movement quality criteria

**Protocol: General Rehabilitation Framework**
1. **Acute Phase (0-5 days):** Rest, ice, compression, elevation (RICE); pain management; gentle range-of-motion work
2. **Early Mobilization Phase (5-14 days):** Progressive active range of motion; begin gentle strengthening in pain-free ranges; address swelling
3. **Progressive Loading Phase (2-6 weeks):** Increase resistance and complexity; restore functional movement patterns; sport-specific preparation
4. **Return-to-Activity Phase (6+ weeks):** Full sport-specific training; durability testing; confidence building

**Rehabilitation Principles:**
- Address root cause, not just symptom
- Restore movement quality before increasing intensity
- Progressively increase time under tension and load
- Incorporate eccentric exercises for tendon health
- Restore local muscle endurance, not just strength
- Test functional patterns under fatigue conditions

## Section 2: Cardio Hierarchy & Volume Recommendations

### 2.1 Cardio Types and Training Hierarchy

**Key Data:**
- Cardio efficacy for fitness goals depends on intensity and duration characteristics
- Different cardio types produce distinct metabolic and cardiovascular adaptations
- Volume recommendations vary dramatically by type (LISS, MISS, HIIT)

**Cardio Type Hierarchy (by research evidence):**

**LISS (Low-Intensity Steady-State):**
- Duration: 30-60+ minutes
- Heart rate: 50-70% max HR
- Weekly volume recommendation: 150-300 minutes
- Benefits: Aerobic base, recovery aid, sustainability, minimal interference with muscle growth
- Drawbacks: Time-intensive; minimal strength/power impact
- Best for: General health, base building, active recovery

**MISS (Moderate-Intensity Steady-State):**
- Duration: 20-45 minutes
- Heart rate: 70-85% max HR
- Weekly volume recommendation: 75-150 minutes
- Benefits: Moderate time efficiency; aerobic and anaerobic threshold development
- Drawbacks: More fatigue than LISS; some interference with strength adaptations
- Best for: Endurance athletes; those seeking time-efficient aerobic work

**HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training):**
- Duration: 15-30 minutes (including recovery)
- Work intervals: 30 seconds to 4 minutes at 85-95%+ max HR
- Recovery intervals: Equal to or longer than work intervals
- Weekly volume recommendation: 60-120 minutes (not more frequent than 2-3x/week)
- Benefits: Maximum time efficiency; enhanced metabolic adaptation; minimal muscle interference when volume controlled
- Drawbacks: High fatigue; CNS demand; requires adequate recovery
- Best for: Time-constrained individuals; metabolic training goals

### 2.2 Cardio-Hypertrophy Interference Effect Thresholds

**Key Data:**
- Cardio volume above threshold levels significantly impairs muscle protein synthesis and hypertrophy
- Interference effect is dose-dependent: higher volumes cause greater suppression
- Timing of cardio relative to strength training modulates the effect
- Adequate protein and caloric intake can partially mitigate interference

**Interference Effect Thresholds:**
- **Safe Zone:** Up to 2-3 sessions/week of LISS (60-120 minutes total); minimal hypertrophy interference
- **Transition Zone:** 3-5 sessions/week of mixed cardio; 1-2 HIIT sessions/week; moderate interference; requires careful nutrition management
- **Problematic Zone:** Daily cardio; 5+ sessions/week; high-volume HIIT (3+ sessions/week); significant suppression of muscle growth

**Specific Thresholds by Modality:**
- LISS: Can tolerate up to 200-300 minutes/week without meaningful hypertrophy loss if nutrition adequate
- MISS: Maximum 150 minutes/week for serious hypertrophy goals
- HIIT: Limited to 2-3 sessions/week maximum; each session counts more heavily against threshold

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- Increase total daily caloric and protein intake (aim for 0.8-1.0g protein/lb bodyweight)
- Separate cardio and strength sessions by 6+ hours
- Prioritize strength training in the morning when neural drive is highest
- Use lower-intensity cardio for most volume; reserve HIIT for 1-2 sessions/week
- Monitor performance metrics in strength training; reduce cardio if strength stalls

### 2.3 When Cardio is Beneficial vs Detrimental

**Key Data:**
- Cardio serves distinct roles depending on training phase and primary goal
- Strategic cardio integration supports both strength and body composition goals
- Excessive cardio volume is one of the most common mistakes in strength training

**When Cardio is BENEFICIAL:**

1. **Off-Season / Base Building Phase**
   - Goal: Aerobic foundation; general health
   - Recommendation: 3-4 sessions/week LISS (45-60 min each)
   - Purpose: Build cardiovascular capacity without interfering with hypertrophy

2. **Deload Weeks**
   - Goal: Active recovery; CNS restoration
   - Recommendation: 2-3 sessions/week LISS (30-45 min)
   - Purpose: Maintain fitness while allowing supercompensation

3. **Maintenance / In-Season (Sports)**
   - Goal: Maintain work capacity; support performance
   - Recommendation: 2-3 sessions/week mixed (LISS/HIIT)
   - Purpose: Prevent detraining while managing fatigue

4. **Body Composition (Fat Loss) Goals**
   - Goal: Maximize fat loss while preserving muscle
   - Recommendation: 3-4 sessions/week LISS/MISS (45-75 min total volume)
   - Purpose: Create caloric deficit with minimal muscle interference
   - **Note:** Cardio should be supplementary to primary caloric deficit; nutrition is primary driver

**When Cardio is DETRIMENTAL:**

1. **Hypertrophy Phase (Primary Goal)**
   - Problem: Excess volume suppresses muscle protein synthesis
   - Recommendation: Limit to 2 sessions/week LISS (60 min max); avoid HIIT
   - Alternative: Use resistance training density and volume as primary stimulus

2. **Strength Development Phase**
   - Problem: Excessive fatigue compromises strength performance
   - Recommendation: Minimal cardio (1-2 sessions/week LISS, 20-30 min max)
   - Alternative: Prioritize CNS recovery via sleep and stress management

3. **High-Volume Strength Training Blocks**
   - Problem: Cumulative fatigue from strength + cardio exceeds recovery capacity
   - Recommendation: Pause cardiovascular training; focus entirely on strength
   - Duration: 4-8 week strength block; resume cardio in next phase

4. **Inadequate Nutrition / Caloric Restriction**
   - Problem: Cardio combined with low calories accelerates muscle loss
   - Recommendation: First increase nutrition; only add cardio if hypercaloric
   - Alternative: Use nutrition and strength training as primary tools

## Section 3: Gut Health Protocols & Nutritional Strategies

### 3.1 Gastrointestinal Tract Anatomy and Digestion Physiology

**Key Data:**
- The GI tract comprises distinct regions (mouth, stomach, small intestine, colon) with specialized functions
- Digestion is fundamentally dependent on mechanical and chemical processes
- Nutrient absorption occurs primarily in the small intestine; colon handles water and fiber
- Stomach acid is critical for protein breakdown and pathogen defense
- Small intestine absorbs most nutrients; colon absorbs water and supports microbiome

**Anatomy Overview:**

**The Mouth:**
- Function: Mechanical breakdown via mastication; chemical breakdown via salivary enzymes (amylase)
- Protocol: Chew thoroughly (25-30 chews per bite); avoid mouth-breathing to preserve saliva
- Implication: Poor chewing increases digestive burden downstream; stress reduces saliva production

**The Stomach:**
- Function: Mechanical churning; acid secretion (HCl); initial protein denaturation
- Key factor: Stomach acid pH (1.5-3.5) is essential for protein breakdown and pathogen kill
- Protocol: Manage stress; avoid frequent PPI/antacid use; consume adequate acid-promoting foods
- Problem: Low stomach acid increases risk of undigested protein, SIBO, and parasite survival

**The Small Intestine (Duodenum, Jejunum, Ileum):**
- Function: Primary site of nutrient absorption (fat, carbs, amino acids, vitamins)
- Anatomy: Jejunum + ileum span ~18 feet; duodenum only 10 inches
- Mechanism: Bile from gallbladder facilitates fat digestion; pancreatic enzymes complete breakdown
- Absorption: Lipids package into chylomicrons; glucose/amino acids enter portal vein to liver
- Critical: Gallbladder removal increases fat intolerance; supplemental bile needed
- Risk: SIBO (bacterial overgrowth) impairs nutrient absorption; symptoms: bloating, diarrhea, malabsorption

**The Colon:**
- Function: Water absorption; fermentation of indigestible carbohydrates (fiber, resistant starch)
- Microbiome: ~100 trillion organisms; predominantly obligate anaerobes (Bifidobacteria, Firmicutes)
- Key: Colonic bacteria produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) from fiber fermentation
- Importance: Butyrate fuels colon cells; maintains barrier integrity; regulates inflammation

### 3.2 Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) Assessment & Protocol

**Key Data:**
- SIBO occurs when bacterial density in small intestine exceeds healthy levels
- Dysbiosis in small intestine impairs nutrient absorption and triggers inflammation
- Symptoms reflect both malabsorption and bacterial fermentation
- Treatment requires addressing root causes, not just antibiotics

**SIBO Diagnosis & Symptoms:**

**Common Symptoms Include:**
- Stomach cramps and bloating
- Diarrhea (frequent, watery)
- Unintended and rapid weight loss
- Indigestion
- Anemia (from iron/B12 malabsorption)

**Risk Factors for SIBO Include:**
- Hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid) from stress or PPI use
- Hypomotility (slow gut transit) from impaired peristalsis
- Compromised immune function
- Irritable bowel disease (IBD)
- Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)

**SIBO Recovery Protocol:**

1. **Restore Upstream Digestive Processes (Foundation):**
   - Manage stress (meditation, breathwork, sleep)
   - Avoid overeating (eat to 80% fullness)
   - Avoid frequent PPI/antacid use; instead support natural acid
   - Supplement with digestive enzymes as needed
   - Use activated charcoal for acute bloating/gas relief

2. **Eliminate Problem Foods:**
   - Use food journaling to identify trigger foods
   - Implement elimination diet for suspected food intolerances
   - Common triggers: High-fat foods, processed foods, excess fructose

3. **Optimize Microbiome (After Acute Recovery):**
   - Prioritize diverse prebiotic sources (resistant starches, fibers, polyphenols)
   - Include fermented foods to support beneficial bacteria
   - Avoid probiotics early (can worsen symptoms); add later after recovery
   - Supplement with HMOs (human milk oligosaccharides) for Bifidobacteria support

### 3.3 Fiber, Polyphenols & Resistant Starch Recommendations

**Key Data:**
- Three prebiotic categories support distinct beneficial bacteria
- Recommended intake: 30-40g total fiber daily from diverse sources
- Polyphenol-rich foods provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits
- Resistant starch is particularly effective for supporting Bifidobacteria and Akkermansia

**Dietary Fiber Classification & Sources:**

**Soluble Fibers:**
- Mechanism: Dissolve in water; slow digestion; feed commensal bacteria
- Examples: Avocados, black beans, citrus, apples, oats
- Benefit: Modest glycemic impact; feeds beneficial bacteria (Bifidobacteria)
- Target: 10-15g daily

**Insoluble Fibers:**
- Mechanism: Provide bulk; accelerate transit; improve stool formation
- Examples: Cauliflower, broccoli, green beans, leafy greens, nuts and seeds
- Benefit: Supports regularity; prevents constipation-related dysbiosis
- Target: 15-25g daily

**Polyphenols (Plant Pigments):**
- Function: Antioxidants; reduce free radicals; anti-inflammatory
- Additional role: Prebiotic that feeds Bifidobacteria
- Examples: Berries, cherries, red cabbage, beets, citrus, red apple skins, olives, black grapes
- Mechanism: Bifidobacteria ferment polyphenols into bioactive metabolites
- Target: Daily consumption of 2-3 polyphenol-rich foods

**Resistant Starch (RS):**
- Definition: Starch that resists digestion by human enzymes
- Mechanism: Fermented in colon; produces short-chain fatty acids (butyrate)
- Benefit: Low glycemic impact; strong prebiotic for Bifidobacteria and Akkermansia
- Examples:
  - Cooked and cooled rice (add 1 tbsp butter/ghee/olive oil to cooking water)
  - Cooked and cooled potatoes (add fat; cool in fridge to boost RS content)
  - Semi-green bananas (raw, not fully ripe)
  - Cooked and cooled oats
  - Raw oats
  - Beans and chickpeas (especially eaten cold)

**Resistant Starch Preparation for Maximum Benefit:**
- **For Rice:** Add fat source to cooking water; cook; cool in refrigerator overnight or 24 hours; reheat (reheating preserves RS). This can reduce glycemic load of white rice significantly.
- **For Potatoes:** Cook potato; cool completely in fridge; serve cold or reheat. Add fat (butter, olive oil, sour cream) to increase palatability and nutrient absorption. Potato salad with vinegar dressing is ideal (acetic acid slows carb digestion).
- **Key:** Cooling is critical; resistant starch forms as starch retrogrades (crystallizes) during cooling

**Target Intake:**
- Total fiber: 30-40g daily minimum; up to 50g optimal
- Polyphenols: 2-3 servings of polyphenol-rich foods daily
- Resistant starch: 15-20g daily (can increase gradually to avoid bloating)

### 3.4 Probiotics, Postbiotics & Synbiotics: Evidence-Based Use

**Key Data:**
- Probiotics alone are insufficient for microbiome restoration
- Specific probiotic strains produce documented benefits; most commercial products are ineffective
- Postbiotics (bacterial metabolites) are the actual bioactive compounds
- Synbiotics (combined pre + probiotics) may offer augmented benefit but require adequate prebiotic support

**Probiotics: Mechanism & Evidence**

**Definition:** Microorganisms that confer benefit to human health

**Critical Context:**
- Colon contains >100 trillion bacteria; most probiotic supplements provide <10 billion organisms
- Probiotic bacteria must be supported by dietary prebiotics to colonize
- Many probiotics lack evidence for claimed benefits
- Specific strains matter: *Saccharomyces boulardii* prevents *C. difficile* infection; most other strains lack equivalent evidence

**Probiotic Post-Antibiotic Use: Important Caution**
- Research (2018, *Cell*): Probiotic consumption post-antibiotics delayed microbiome reconstitution by 6 months
- Mechanism: Probiotic bacteria inhibit growth of other beneficial bacteria
- Better approach: Prioritize diverse prebiotic sources instead
- Timeline: Wait 2-4 weeks post-antibiotics; then emphasize fermented foods and prebiotics

**Recommended Strategy if Considering Probiotics:**
1. Prioritize diverse prebiotic intake first (resistant starches, fibers, polyphenols, HMOs)
2. If using probiotics, ensure sufficient prebiotic "food" to support colonization
3. Avoid post-antibiotic probiotic use; instead use targeted prebiotic support
4. Select strains with documented evidence (not all probiotics are equal)

**Source:** Gut-Strong-eBook.pdf, Pages 35-37

### 3.5 Postbiotics: The Key Bacterial Metabolites

**Key Data:**
- Postbiotics are bioactive metabolites produced by probiotics when they consume prebiotics
- Postbiotics (not the bacteria themselves) drive health benefits
- Fermented foods are rich in postbiotics
- Specific postbiotic (urolithin A) shows dramatic benefits for mitochondrial and muscular health

**Postbiotic Definition & Mechanism:**

**What are Postbiotics?**
- Factors synthesized by probiotics as they ferment prebiotics
- Types include short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), enzymes, metabolites
- Research (Stanford): Fermented foods increase microbiome diversity via postbiotic content, not probiotic content per se

**Key Postbiotic: Urolithin A**
- Source: Produced by Bifidobacteria upon consumption of ellagic acid (found in pomegranate, blackberries, other red fruits)
- Benefits documented:
  - Enhanced mitochondrial health and energy production
  - Improved muscular recovery
  - Enhanced brain health and cognitive function
  - Anti-aging properties via autophagy stimulation
- Availability: Now available as standalone supplement for those who cannot convert ellagic acid adequately

**Postbiotic Production Strategy:**
1. Consume prebiotic-rich foods (fibers, resistant starches, polyphenols)
2. Consume fermented foods (naturally high in postbiotics)
3. Support Bifidobacteria through HMO supplementation if needed
4. Test microbiome response via sequential stool tests if desired

### 3.6 Key Microbiome Members: Bifidobacteria & Akkermansia

**Key Data:**
- Two bacterial genera (Bifidobacteria and Akkermansia) are critical for microbiome and metabolic health
- Both are depleted in inflammatory and metabolic diseases
- Specific dietary and lifestyle factors enhance these protective bacteria
- Genetic factors (FUT2 gene) influence Bifidobacteria baseline levels

**BIFIDOBACTERIA: The Foundation Bacterium**

**Function:**
- Obligate anaerobes (require low-oxygen colon environment)
- Ferment indigestible carbohydrates into SCFAs (acetate, propionate, butyrate)
- Support butyrate-producing bacteria through cross-feeding
- Crucial for immune system development (especially in infants)

**Health Impact:**
- High Bifidobacteria = diverse microbiome; anti-inflammatory environment
- Decline with age (associated with aging-related diseases)
- Low levels increase risk of IBD, IBS, and autoimmune conditions
- Formula-fed infants have lower Bifidobacteria; breastfed infants have >90% (due to HMOs)

**Genetic Consideration: FUT2 Gene**
- FUT2 secretor status determines carbohydrate availability for Bifidobacteria
- **"Secretors" (GA or GG genotype):** Express carbohydrates on intestinal cell surface; naturally higher Bifidobacteria
- **"Non-Secretors" (AA genotype):** ~20% of population; lower baseline Bifidobacteria; increased risk of IBD/IBS
- Non-secretors should prioritize aggressive prebiotic support (HMOs, diverse fibers, resistant starches)

**Supporting Bifidobacteria:**
- Dietary: HMOs, diverse fibers, resistant starches, polyphenols, fermented foods
- Lifestyle: Sleep, stress management, avoid frequent antibiotics
- Supplementation: HMOs (human milk oligosaccharides) if diet insufficient

**AKKERMANSIA: The Barrier Defender**

**Function:**
- Gram-negative anaerobe residing in mucus layer of colon
- Feeds on carbohydrate groups (glycoproteins) from mucus
- Stimulates mucus-producing Goblet cells to secrete more protective mucus
- Maintains intestinal barrier integrity
- Depleted in obesity and metabolic diseases

**Metabolic & Health Implications:**
- **Among the strongest correlations with metabolic health** currently identified
- Lean individuals have significantly higher Akkermansia levels
- Deficiency: Associated with poor glycemic control, elevated lipids, increased gut permeability
- Repletion: Shown to improve insulin sensitivity, reduce inflammation, enhance barrier function
- >5% of healthy adult microbiome = Akkermansia typical; obesity associates with <1%

**Supporting Akkermansia:**
- **Dietary:** Red polyphenols (blueberries, blackberries, cranberries, red cabbage), HMOs, resistant starches
- **Supplementation:** HMOs (particularly effective), berberine (stimulates mucin secretion)
- **Lifestyle:** Exercise (endurance particularly effective); intermittent fasting; avoid high-fat diet

**Lifestyle Strategies for Akkermansia Enhancement:**
- **Exercise:** Endurance training significantly increases Akkermansia populations
- **Intermittent Fasting:** Both short (16-hour) and longer fasting periods boost Akkermansia
- **Diet:** Emphasize red polyphenols; avoid processed high-fat foods

**Inflammation Detection via Akkermansia:**
- If gassy or bloated after HMO or prebiotic consumption: Likely gut inflammation present
- Strategy: Implement 1-7 day liquid diet (bone broth, smoothies, blended soups) while consuming polyphenols
- This reduces intestinal burden while supporting anti-inflammatory metabolites
- Then gradually reintroduce solid foods with prebiotic support

### 3.7 Colon Health Screening & Assessment Modalities

**Key Data:**
- Multiple assessment tools available to evaluate microbiome composition and colon health
- Microbiome sequencing is still evolving; interpretation varies
- Colonoscopy is gold standard for structural evaluation but disrupts microbiome
- Fecal immunochemical testing (FIT) is non-invasive screening for disease

**Assessment Modalities:**

**Microbiome Sequencing:**
- Purpose: Profile bacterial composition (Bifidobacteria, Akkermansia, diversity)
- Limitation: Still in infancy; "healthy" ranges vary significantly between individuals
- Application: Monitor changes in response to dietary/lifestyle interventions
- Timing: Can be used serially to track microbiome response to prebiotic/lifestyle changes

**Colonoscopy:**
- Purpose: Visualize colon structure; identify polyps, inflammation, cancer
- Standard: Begin at age 45-75 (average risk); age 35 if family history (by age 45)
- Frequency: Every 1-3 years depending on findings; negative result = every 1-3 years
- Important: Colonoscopy prep damages microbiome; recovery takes weeks. Post-procedure, use aggressive prebiotic strategy (as above: diverse fibers, resistant starches, polyphenols, HMOs)

**Fecal Immunochemical Test (FIT):**
- Purpose: Non-invasive screen for microscopic blood in stool
- Use: Low-risk individuals without family history; sensitive for colorectal cancer
- Advantage: No invasive procedure; can repeat annually
- Limitation: Cannot visualize structure; only detects bleeding

**Recommended Screening Strategy (Average Risk):**
- Age 45-50: Baseline colonoscopy to establish colon status
- If negative: Repeat colonoscopy per gastroenterologist recommendation (typically 5-10 years)
- Between colonoscopies: FIT annually if preferred (non-invasive alternative)
- Family history of early-onset colon cancer: Earlier screening and increased frequency

**Post-Colonoscopy Recovery Protocol:**
- Day 1-3: Focus on prebiotic reintroduction (similar to acute inflammation protocol above)
- Days 3-7: Gradual return to full diet; continue emphasizing diverse fibers and polyphenols
- Ongoing: Aggressive prebiotic support (HMOs, resistant starches, polyphenols) for 2-4 weeks post-procedure

### 3.8 Comprehensive GI Health Protocol Integration

**Key Data:**
- Optimal GI health requires integrated attention to all regions (mouth, stomach, small intestine, colon)
- No single intervention fixes dysbiosis; root-cause resolution is essential
- Long-term adherence to prebiotic-focused nutrition is more effective than cyclical protocols

**Integrated Protocol Summary by GI Region:**

**THE MOUTH:**
- Chew food thoroughly (25-30 chews per bite)
- Avoid mouth-breathing; preserve saliva production
- Monitor stimulant use (caffeine, nicotine)

**THE STOMACH:**
- Manage stress (primary driver of reduced acid secretion)
- Avoid overeating (eat to 80% fullness)
- Consume prebiotic fiber, resistant starches, polyphenols alongside dietary protein
- Avoid frequent PPI/antacid use; allow natural digestive processes when possible

**THE SMALL INTESTINE:**
- Prevent SIBO through adequate stomach acid and stress management
- Avoid frequent unnecessary antibiotics
- Optimize fat digestion: adequate bile (supplement if gallbladder removed)
- Don't overuse proton pump inhibitors or antacids

**THE COLON:**
- Consume adequate polyphenols; they serve as antioxidants and inflammation fighters
- Consume daily requirement for dietary fibers (~30-40g minimum)
- Consume plenty of resistant starches; support good bacteria and healthy microbiome
- Consume fermented foods (particularly after antibiotic use)
- Get colon health screening as needed or as desired

**Holistic Checklist for Colon Health:**
- [ ] Reduce inflammation (stress management, adequate sleep, polyphenol-rich diet)
- [ ] Consume fermented foods (especially post-antibiotic)
- [ ] Optimize microbiome with prebiotics (fibers, resistant starches, polyphenols)
- [ ] Implement colon health screening as needed/desired
